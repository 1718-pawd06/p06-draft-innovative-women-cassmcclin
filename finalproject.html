Biography

Alice Paul was born in New Jersey and attended Swarthmore College before living in England and pushing for women's voting rights. She returned to America in 1910 and became a leader in the suffragist movement. Paul began her full-time suffrage career in 1912 and joined the National American Woman Suffrage Association (NAWSA). There, she gathered a group of young women who were willing to depart from the association's conservative tactics. The group of women officially broke away from NAWSA in 1914, and in 1916, she founded the National Women's Party (NWP) Going against public demand, Paul and the NWP continued to picket the White House and Congress after the United States entered World War I. Paul and some of the other "Silent Sentenials" were arrested under the charge of "obstructing traffic" even though they were using their Constitutional right to peacefully protest on the streets.


Just because women finally had the right to vote doesn't mean Alice Paul was planning on stopping her campaign for women anytime soon. She earned a law degree from Washington College and a master's and doctorate's degrees from American University. She continued to fight for an Equal Rights Amendment up until she died, 50 years later. It was finally passed by Congress in 1972 but never ratified by enough states. Alice Paul died 5 years later of a stroke.

Accomplishments

  • Activist for Women's Right to Vote
  • Earned a Ph.D from the University of Pennsylvania in 1912
  • Co-founder of Congressional Union for Woman Suffrage
  • Organized the "Silent Sentenials" group, which held banners that had messages directed at the Woodrow administration. They stood outside the White House, regardless of weather, for 2.5 years.
  • Went on a hunger strike in the Occoquan Workhouse, and later revealed the abuse her and the women of the NWP suffered there, which turned many to support their cause.
  • Her efforts led to the ratification of the 19th Amendment to the U.S. Constitution- the right of U.S. citizens to vote shall not be denied on the basis of sex
  • Pushing for an Equal Rights Amendment
